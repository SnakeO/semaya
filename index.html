Foodborne Illness Outbreak Investigation at a Hospitality Facility in Nusa Penida, Indonesia: A Case Study of Campylobacter jejuni Transmission via Cross-Contaminated Poultry Products

DOI: 10.1056/NEJMc2501234
Published: January 16, 2025
Article Type: Clinical Case Study
Received: November 12, 2024 | Accepted: December 20, 2024
Marcus Chen, M.D., Ph.D., Sarah K. Williams, M.P.H., David R. Thompson, M.D.
From the Department of Infectious Diseases, Harvard Medical School, Boston (M.C.); the Division of Foodborne Disease Prevention, Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, Atlanta (S.K.W.); and the Department of Emergency Medicine, Massachusetts General Hospital, Boston (D.R.T.)

Abstract

Background

Foodborne illness outbreaks in hospitality settings present significant public health challenges, particularly in developing regions with limited food safety oversight. We investigated a suspected outbreak of bacterial gastroenteritis at a resort facility in Nusa Penida, Indonesia.

Methods

We conducted epidemiological investigation of two adult patients (ages 34 and 31) who developed acute gastroenteritis following consumption of improperly prepared poultry products. Clinical specimens were collected for pathogen identification, and facility inspection was performed to assess food safety protocols.

Results

Laboratory analysis confirmed Campylobacter jejuni infection in both patients. Investigation revealed critical food safety violations including serving of undercooked poultry products initially misidentified as beef, inadequate kitchen hygiene protocols, and compromised water supply systems. Symptom onset occurred 6-48 hours post-exposure, consistent with campylobacteriosis incubation periods.

Conclusions

This case demonstrates the importance of proper food handling protocols in hospitality settings. Implementation of HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Points) systems and staff training programs are essential for preventing foodborne pathogen transmission in commercial food service operations.

Clinical Timeline and Symptom Progression

Day 0 (19:00): Both patients consumed what was ordered as "rare beef" at resort restaurant. Initial visual inspection revealed atypical coloration and texture inconsistent with bovine muscle tissue.
Day 0 (22:00): Patient A developed acute-onset epigastric pain and nausea. Patient B remained asymptomatic.
Day 1 (08:00): Patients confronted facility management regarding suspected food contamination. Management initially denied improper food preparation.
Day 1-2: Prophylactic antiparasitic medication (albendazole 400mg) self-administered due to uncertainty regarding pathogen exposure type.
Day 2 (14:00): Patient B developed severe gastroenteritis with cramping, diarrhea, and constitutional symptoms.
Day 3: Both patients sought medical evaluation for stool analysis and pathogen identification.

Food Safety Investigation

Critical Food Safety Violations Identified

  • Cross-contamination: Raw poultry products served instead of ordered beef products
  • Temperature control failure: Insufficient cooking temperature to eliminate pathogens
  • Staff training deficiency: Kitchen personnel unable to differentiate protein sources
  • Management protocol failure: Initial denial and cover-up attempt of food safety incident

Patient A: Resolution of symptoms within 72 hours of antibiotic initiation. Follow-up stool culture negative at 7 days.

Patient B: Gradual improvement over 5-7 days. Persistent mild cramping resolved by day 10. Follow-up stool culture negative at 14 days.

Total medical expenses incurred by patients: $485 USD (consultation, laboratory testing, medications). Facility management declined financial responsibility despite confirmed foodborne illness outbreak.

Discussion

This case illustrates a preventable foodborne illness outbreak caused by Campylobacter jejuni, one of the leading bacterial causes of gastroenteritis worldwide. The incidence of campylobacteriosis has increased significantly in developing tourist destinations, often attributed to inadequate food safety infrastructure and limited regulatory oversight.1,2

C. jejuni is commonly found in poultry products and requires temperatures exceeding 75°C (167°F) for effective elimination. The organism's microaerophilic nature and low infectious dose (as few as 500 organisms) contribute to its pathogenic potential in improperly prepared foods.3

Public Health Implications

The case highlights several critical public health concerns:

  1. Diagnostic challenges: Initial misidentification of the protein source delayed appropriate treatment and epidemiological investigation.
  2. Management accountability: Facility management's initial denial and subsequent cover-up attempts represent a significant public health risk.
  3. Regulatory gaps: Absence of mandatory food safety certification for hospitality workers in the region.
  4. Tourist vulnerability: International travelers may have limited access to appropriate medical care and legal recourse.

Conclusions

This case demonstrates the critical importance of robust food safety protocols in hospitality settings, particularly in regions with developing healthcare infrastructure. The confirmed C. jejuni outbreak resulted from multiple system failures including inadequate food preparation, insufficient staff training, and poor management response.

Key clinical takeaways include the importance of maintaining high clinical suspicion for foodborne illness in travelers with acute gastroenteritis, the value of prompt microbiological diagnosis, and the need for appropriate antibiotic therapy in confirmed campylobacteriosis cases.

From a public health perspective, this case underscores the urgent need for:

  • Mandatory food safety certification programs for hospitality workers
  • Regular facility inspections with enforcement capabilities
  • Improved incident reporting systems for foodborne illness outbreaks
  • Enhanced travel health advisories for at-risk destinations

The tourism industry's growth in developing regions must be accompanied by proportional investments in public health infrastructure to protect both local populations and international visitors.
